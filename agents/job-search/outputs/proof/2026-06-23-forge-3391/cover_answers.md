## What are you motivated by?

I'm motivated by finding the load-bearing inefficiency in a complex system and eliminating it. Not incrementally improving it, actually replacing it. At Microsoft, that meant taking a recovery validation program that two people were running manually and turning it into a platformized system running 45+ enterprise drills a year across partners like Walmart, Databricks, and SAP. The scale change wasn't the point, it was that the old way was a ceiling and the new way removed it.

I'm also motivated by the moment when a customer sees something they assumed required a team of people get done automatically, correctly, at scale. I built an internal AI agent that cut drill planning cycle time by 39% and expanded capacity by 21%. The technical work was interesting, but what stuck with me was that it changed what the team thought was possible. That's the kind of outcome I want to own.

Longer term, I want to work at the frontier of how software actually gets built and deployed. The idea that natural language and structured control flow can replace entire categories of manual enterprise work, that's not an incremental bet. It's a fundamental shift, and I want to be the person delivering it to the companies that need it most.

## What is something hard or exceptional that you've done?

At Microsoft, I was asked to deliver Azure's first rack-level disaster recovery drill program. There was no playbook, no prior art internally, and the customer was a Fortune 500 partner with C-suite visibility on the outcome. I had four months.

I started with deep process discovery, breaking down what a rack-level failure actually looks like in production, mapping every dependency, every handoff, every assumption the operations and engineering teams were making. Most of those assumptions were undocumented. I turned them into a precise, step-by-step execution model and then built the coordination layer around it, aligning 14 cross-org teams under a single recovery framework. The drill ran on time and hit a 94% recovery rate on the first execution.

What made it hard wasn't the technical complexity, it was the ambiguity. Nobody could tell me what success looked like because nobody had done it before. I had to define the outcome, build the process to get there, and hold the stakeholders to it from ops teams up to executive leadership. That combination, owning the full arc from discovery to production outcome under real business pressure, is exactly the kind of work I want to do more of.
