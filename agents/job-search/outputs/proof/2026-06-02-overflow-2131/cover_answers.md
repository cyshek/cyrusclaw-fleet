## Describe a complex product or technical concept you had to explain to a non-technical customer. How did you approach it?

At Microsoft, I worked on a sovereign-cloud isolation test tied to a $1.5B+ enterprise contract, and the hardest part wasn't the engineering, it was getting non-technical stakeholders to understand what network isolation and distributed-systems failure recovery actually meant for their business. The customer didn't care about the mechanics of how we isolated a region. They cared about whether their data stayed protected and their workloads stayed up. So I reframed everything around that. Instead of walking through architecture diagrams, I led with the outcome they wanted, then explained the technical concept only as far as it was needed to build confidence in that outcome.

My approach is pretty consistent: start with the business risk or goal in their language, use a concrete analogy for the technical mechanism, then show evidence. In this case I tied our recovery work to a clear number, a 94% recovery rate across drills, because a single trustworthy metric does more to build confidence than ten minutes of jargon. When I sense someone nodding along but not really following, I slow down and check understanding rather than pushing forward.

I did a lot of this during my internships too, running customer-facing demos and training sessions for AI code-generation workflows across 14 teams. The pattern that works for me is translating the technical concept into 'here's what changes for you and why it matters,' and saving the deeper detail for the people who actually want it. That balance of clarity for non-technical buyers and real technical depth for engineers is exactly what I'd want to bring to discovery calls and onboarding at Overflow.
