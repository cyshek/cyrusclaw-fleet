Honestly, that's the version of this work I find most interesting. Pulling from a backlog means someone else already defined the problem; the leverage is usually in the gap between what operators are actually doing and what anyone thought to write down as a ticket. I find that gap by being in the room.

The closest direct parallel: early in my time at Microsoft I sat with the teams running Azure resilience drills and watched the planning process before touching anything. What I found was that drill scheduling and coordination were being done manually across dozens of stakeholders, with no shared state and no automation, even though the underlying logic was completely repeatable. I built an internal AI agent for drill planning and restructured the automation workflows around it. Planning cycle time dropped 39% and drill capacity increased 21%. The number that mattered to leadership was capacity, and that moved within weeks of shipping.

A similar thing happened during my 2022 internship: I embedded with 20+ service teams, mapped 81 hours of manual toil per region launch, and built the automation prioritization framework that translated that map into a concrete roadmap. The outcome was a 28% faster region launch timeline and $3M in accelerated revenue. Both times the problem was invisible until I was close enough to the actual work to see it.
