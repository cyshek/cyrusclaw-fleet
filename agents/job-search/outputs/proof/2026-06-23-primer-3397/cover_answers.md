## Why are you interested in Primer, and this role in particular?

A lot of my work at Microsoft has been about exactly the problem Primer is trying to solve operationally: how do you scale operations without scaling headcount proportionally? I built an internal Resilience Automation Platform that reduced operational toil by 30% and transitioned a 2-person manual process into a self-service system sustaining 45+ annual drills. I also built an AI agent that cut drill planning time by 39% and increased capacity by 21% - the explicit goal was freeing operators to focus on higher-judgment work rather than repetitive scheduling. That framing, giving time back to the people doing the real work, is exactly what this role is about. The idea that every minute returned to a teacher is a minute spent with kids is the same logic, just in a context that matters even more.

What draws me to Primer specifically is the seriousness of the product challenge. PrimerOS is not a productivity tool bolted onto an existing institution - it is the operational backbone of a new kind of school network, and the design decisions made now will shape how hundreds of campuses run for years. I am also drawn to the constraint: affordability depends on operational efficiency, which means the product work is not optional or nice-to-have, it is load-bearing. That kind of stakes makes the craft matter. I want to work on problems where getting it right actually changes outcomes for kids and teachers, and Primer is one of the few places building at that intersection of real physical operations, AI, and mission.

## What's something you've built, led, or improved that you're most proud of?

The project I keep coming back to is the Resilience Automation Platform I built at Microsoft. When I started, recovery validation was a 2-person manual operation - every drill required hand-scheduling, custom coordination, and a lot of operator time just keeping the process moving. I took it from that state to a platformized system with self-service scheduling, standardized workflows, and enough capacity to sustain 45+ drills annually. The business impact came out to $14M+, but the part I am actually proud of is the structural change: the work that used to require dedicated people running it manually now runs as a repeatable system, and the operators who were buried in logistics can focus on the higher-judgment pieces.

What made it hard was that the underlying workflows were genuinely messy - hardware dependencies, cross-org coordination, sovereign cloud constraints, and execution happening under executive visibility with real contract implications. I had to spend a lot of time watching how the work actually happened before I could figure out what to systematize and what to leave alone. That instinct, sitting with the reality of the operation before designing anything, is the same thing I would bring to understanding how teachers and campus operators actually work at Primer. The best product decisions I have made came from that kind of fieldwork, not from assumptions.
