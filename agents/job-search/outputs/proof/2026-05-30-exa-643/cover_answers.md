# Cover answers — Exa, Forward Deployed Engineer (exa-8932d319-b456-4ee0-9efd-16d82be8de80)

## Why are you interested in working at Exa?

Search is the substrate that agents are going to run on, and the incumbents weren't built for that workload. I've spent the last two years at Microsoft sitting between enterprise customers (Databricks, Walmart, SAP) and the engineers shipping the platform, and the work I'm proudest of is when I can take a customer pain point on a Monday call and have a fix or a tutorial in their hands by Friday. The FDE role is literally that loop, just with a faster company and a more interesting technical bet.

Specifically on Exa: I've been following the neural search vs. keyword approach since the Metaphor days, and the thesis that agents need a different retrieval substrate than humans do feels obviously right. I want to be the person turning that API into real revenue and writing the demos that make developers go 'oh, that's the unlock.'

## What's something you worked on that you were proud of?

At Microsoft I pioneered Azure's first proactive rack-level resilience testing program. When I picked it up, the idea existed on a slide; four months later we had a working program running drills at a 94% recovery rate and surfacing real hardware defects that Fortune 500 customers had been flagging for months.

The part I'm most proud of isn't the metric, it's that I had to do every job to get there: write the PRD, sit on calls with skeptical hardware engineers, prototype the scheduling APIs, demo it to execs, and then onboard partner teams with tutorials and runbooks. It was the closest thing to running a tiny startup inside a giant company, and it convinced me I want to do the full-stack customer-plus-code thing for real.

Also worth mentioning: I built an internal AI agent on top of LLM tooling to automate the drill planning workflow, which cut planning cycle time 39% and got picked up by partner teams. That one taught me how much leverage you get from shipping a small, sharp tool to the right user.

## What motivates you?

Short feedback loops with real users. The work that lights me up is the kind where I talk to a customer in the morning, ship something in the afternoon, and watch them use it the next day. My Pro Painters stint (scoping 200+ proposals a month, owning outbound to close) and my Microsoft work (translating enterprise pain into backend fixes in the same sprint) are technically very different jobs, but the motivation is identical: get close enough to the user that you can't hide behind process.

The second thing is leverage. I built an AI agent for drill planning that a few partner teams now use, and that compounding feeling, where one weekend of code keeps paying off, is addictive. Search for agents is the same shape of bet at a much bigger scale.

And honestly, I want to be around people who are obsessed with the problem. The 'low-ego, high-agency' framing in the JD is the team I've been trying to find.
