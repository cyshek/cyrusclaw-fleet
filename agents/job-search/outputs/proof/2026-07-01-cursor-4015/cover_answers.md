## Tell us about a time you convinced a technical audience to adopt a new solution or tool. What was the resistance and how did you handle it?

During my first internship at Microsoft in 2023, I led adoption of an AI-driven code generation tool across 14 Azure engineering teams. The resistance was real and pretty consistent: engineers felt the tool added friction to their existing workflows and weren't convinced the output quality justified the context-switching cost. A few senior engineers were openly skeptical, which made the broader team reluctant to experiment.

Rather than pushing top-down, I ran hands-on demo sessions tailored to each team's actual codebase and pain points. I wasn't showing a generic pitch deck - I was sitting with developers, running the tool on their real tasks, and letting the results speak. When the tool underperformed on something, I acknowledged it directly instead of deflecting. That candor built more trust than any slide would have. I also gathered structured feedback from 11+ user interviews and fed that back into the product roadmap, so engineers could see their concerns were shaping what got built next.

By the end of the internship, the tool was saving 37 engineering hours monthly across those teams and had moved from a skeptic-heavy pilot to a workflow teams were actually relying on. The biggest lesson was that technical audiences don't need you to oversell - they need you to show up prepared, be honest about tradeoffs, and make it easy to get value fast. That same approach is exactly what I'd bring to rolling out Cursor inside complex enterprise engineering organizations.
