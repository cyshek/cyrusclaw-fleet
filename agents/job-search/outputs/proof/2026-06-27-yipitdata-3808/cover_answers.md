## Tell me about an AI-powered product you owned recently that you are most proud of. What was the product, which AI technologies were used, and what was your role in bringing it to market?

The product I'm most proud of is the LLM-powered drill planning tool I owned at Microsoft. The problem was that planning a resilience drill across a large enterprise customer environment was a manual, time-intensive process - teams were stitching together inputs from multiple systems and making judgment calls that slowed down the entire cycle. My role was to own the product strategy end to end: define the requirements, work with engineering on the AI architecture and prompt design, run discovery with the teams doing the planning work, and establish how we'd evaluate whether the outputs were actually trustworthy enough to act on.

The core technology was an LLM integrated into the planning workflow, structured to take in operational context and produce a prioritized, actionable drill plan. Getting the output to be reliable enough for enterprise use - where a bad recommendation has real consequences - was the hardest part. I worked closely with engineering on the evaluation framework, iterating on prompt structure and output formatting until accuracy and consistency hit a bar I was confident shipping. The result was a 39% reduction in planning cycle time and a 21% expansion in drill capacity across customers including Databricks, Walmart, SAP, and NetApp. What makes me proud of it isn't just the numbers - it's that we built something operators actually trusted and used daily.
