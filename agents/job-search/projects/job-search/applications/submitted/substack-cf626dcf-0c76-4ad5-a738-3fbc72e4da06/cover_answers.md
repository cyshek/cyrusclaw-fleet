## Why Substack?

My work at Microsoft has been almost entirely about the same core challenge this role is built around: taking bespoke requests from sophisticated enterprise partners like Databricks, Walmart, and SAP, and translating them into reusable platform capabilities rather than one-off solutions. I built an internal Resilience Automation Platform that did exactly that, synthesizing partner needs into self-service primitives that scaled across the org and reduced operational toil by 30%. I also owned GTM end-to-end for everything the team shipped, including a rack-level drill program I took from zero to production in four months. That combination of platform thinking, partner empathy, and go-to-market ownership is what this role is asking for, and it maps directly to how I've been operating.

What draws me to Substack specifically is the bet it's making on the creator economy as a durable business model, not a feature of something else. The enterprise creator segment is a genuinely interesting forcing function: these are publishers and editorial operations pushing ahead of the product, which means the PM role here is less about incremental optimization and more about identifying what scalable primitives need to exist before the market demands them. That's the kind of problem I want to be working on. Substack's model, giving creators ownership, direct audience relationships, and a real revenue stream, is one I think matters, and building the platform infrastructure that makes it work at the enterprise tier is a compelling place to spend time.
