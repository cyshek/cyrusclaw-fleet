## What are you motivated by?

I'm motivated by finding the load-bearing manual process inside a complex organization and replacing it with something that actually scales. The work that excites me most isn't incremental polish - it's the moment you identify that a team is spending thousands of hours on something that shouldn't require a human at all, and then you build the thing that makes that true.

At Microsoft, I kept landing in situations where the right answer wasn't to optimize the existing process but to rethink it entirely. Building an internal AI agent that cut drill planning time by 39%, or converting a 2-person manual recovery operation into a platformized deployment system - those outcomes mattered because they changed how the team operated, not just how fast they moved. That distinction drives me.

I also care about the customer side of this. Discovery work - sitting with an ops team, mapping out exactly how they do something painful, and then translating that into something automatable - is genuinely one of my favorite parts of the job. Getting that right requires understanding the workflow at a level most people don't bother with, and the results are proportionally better when you do.

## What is something hard or exceptional that you've done?

The hardest thing I've done professionally was standing up Azure's first proactive rack-level resilience testing capability from scratch, under production constraints, with no playbook to follow. The challenge wasn't just technical - it was convincing teams to let us run failure tests against live infrastructure before anyone had seen evidence it was worth the risk.

I had to do deep process discovery across engineering, operations, and customer-facing teams to understand exactly what failure modes existed and what a recovery workflow needed to look like at each layer. Then I had to architect a pilot that would surface real signal - hardware defects, recovery gaps - without causing the outages we were trying to prevent. Over a 4-month pilot, we hit a 94% recovery rate and surfaced critical hardware defects that wouldn't have been caught otherwise. That capability got adopted org-wide.

What made it genuinely hard was the combination of technical precision and stakeholder navigation required simultaneously. I was running 14 cross-org recovery executions under C-suite visibility while also doing the hands-on workflow design and building internal tooling to reduce planning overhead by 39%. One of those engagements was directly tied to a $1.5B+ enterprise contract, where I served as the bridge lead for a sovereign-cloud network isolation deployment. The margin for error was effectively zero, and there was no established process to fall back on.
