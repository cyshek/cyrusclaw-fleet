These weren't passive check-ins - I was the person in the room architecting the solution and accountable for the outcome.

Beyond execution, I've consistently driven product direction through direct customer discovery. During my 2023 Microsoft internship, I ran 11+ customer interviews with Azure service teams to surface feature gaps, and those conversations directly shaped roadmap priorities - intent-based YAML generation got added as a prioritized use case because of what I heard in those sessions. In my 2022 internship, I ran discovery with 20+ service teams, identified 81 hours of manual toil per region launch, and translated that into a product roadmap. That pattern - go deep with the customer, find the real pain, then build something that actually fixes it - is how I've operated throughout.

At Pro Painters earlier in my career, I owned the end-to-end sales workflow redesign, which meant managing the customer relationship through a 200+ proposal pipeline and directly reducing handoff errors across the post-sale lifecycle. The contexts are different, but the muscle is the same: talk to the people doing the work, understand where things break down, and build something that changes the outcome.

## Why are you interested in Actively AI?

The work I find most meaningful at Microsoft has been building AI-powered workflows that replace manual processes at scale - the drill planning agent I built cut planning cycle time by 39% and increased throughput by 21%, and scaling that from a 2-person operation to a platformized system handling 45+ annual drills is exactly the kind of structural change Actively is describing. The framing of "per-account agents working 24/7 without being asked" maps directly to what I've been trying to build inside Azure - the idea that you don't need a human triaging every decision if the agent logic is sound and the workflow is well-designed.

What draws me specifically to Actively is that you're not just building tooling on top of existing GTM processes - you're making the case that the structural bottleneck is human capacity and then solving for that directly. That's a harder and more interesting problem than incrementally improving rep efficiency. The fact that the founding team comes from Stanford AI research, and that the customer base already includes companies like Ramp and Samsara, tells me the technical bar and the go-to-market motion are both serious. I want to be in a place where I'm writing the playbook, not following one, and this role - owning customer accounts end-to-end while shaping the product from frontline insights - is exactly that.
