## Tell us about a time you convinced a technical audience to adopt a new solution or tool. What was the resistance and how did you handle it?

During my first internship at Microsoft, I was tasked with driving adoption of AI-native code generation workflows across 14 Azure engineering teams. The resistance was real and reasonable: engineers had already built their own ad hoc scripts, were skeptical of reliability in production contexts, and didn't want to slow down active sprints to learn a new tool. A few team leads were openly dismissive in early conversations.

I didn't push the tool top-down. Instead, I ran discovery calls with each team to understand where they were actually losing time, then mapped the workflow directly to those pain points before any demo. I did live, hands-on walkthroughs using their own codebases and their own examples, not canned demos. When something didn't work the way they expected, I stayed in the room and iterated on prompts and configuration in real time rather than deflecting. That built credibility faster than any slide deck.

The outcome was 14 teams actively using the workflow in production, saving 37 engineering hours monthly. The bigger lesson I took from it: technical audiences adopt things when they trust that you understand their actual constraints and aren't just selling. You have to go deep in their code, surface the real friction, and show - not tell - that the solution fits. That's the same approach I've taken at every stage since, including when I built the AI agent for drill planning at Microsoft that cut planning cycle time by 39% across cross-functional teams who were initially resistant to changing a process that had been manual for years.
