## Tell us about a time you convinced a technical audience to adopt a new solution or tool. What was the resistance and how did you handle it?

During my Microsoft internship in 2023, I was tasked with driving adoption of AI-powered workflow tooling across 14 Azure engineering teams. The resistance was real and varied - some teams were skeptical about accuracy, others didn't want to change tooling mid-sprint, and a few leads wanted to see hard evidence before committing any engineer time to it. Generic demos weren't going to cut it.

I ran 11+ structured discovery sessions with individual service teams first, before pitching anything. The goal was to understand exactly where their pain was - what tasks were eating time, where errors crept in, what they actually cared about. That gave me enough specificity to tailor each conversation to their workflow rather than presenting a one-size solution. I also surfaced 11+ concrete feature gaps back to the product team, which helped teams feel heard rather than sold to. Once a few leads saw the tooling shaped around their actual problems, adoption spread more naturally.

The outcome was 37 engineering hours saved monthly across those teams, and the intent-based YAML generation use case I surfaced in those sessions got pushed onto the product roadmap. The lesson I took from it: technical audiences don't resist good tools, they resist tools that feel generic or unproven. Discovery first, pitch second.
