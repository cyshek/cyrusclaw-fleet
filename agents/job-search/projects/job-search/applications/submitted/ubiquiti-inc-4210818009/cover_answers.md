# Cover answers — Ubiquiti, Pre-Sales Engineer (ubiquiti-inc-4210818009)

## Tell us about a time you had to deeply understand a technical product or system and then convince a skeptical customer or stakeholder to adopt it. What did you do, and what was the outcome?

At Microsoft I built Azure's first proactive resilience testing capability, a rack-level drill program that deliberately fails parts of the infrastructure to validate recovery. The hard part wasn't the engineering. It was convincing service teams who were skeptical about intentionally injecting faults into systems tied to enterprise contracts. They saw risk; I needed them to see the value of finding defects in a controlled lab environment instead of during a real outage.

So I went deep on the actual recovery behavior of their systems, then translated that complex distributed-systems detail into clear narratives each team could act on. Instead of asking for blanket buy-in, I ran controlled drills, surfaced critical hardware defects they hadn't known about, and showed a 94% recovery rate. Concrete results did the convincing that arguments couldn't.

The program landed in four months and I went on to direct 14 cross-org recovery executions under executive visibility, including a sovereign-cloud network isolation test tied to a $1.5B+ enterprise contract. The teams that were most skeptical early became the ones requesting drills, because they'd seen it protect them rather than expose them.

## Describe a period where you went far beyond your defined role or normal working hours to achieve a result you cared about. What drove you, and what did you actually do?

When I was scaling Azure's recovery validation, the work was a manual, 2-person operation that couldn't keep up with demand. Nobody asked me to rebuild it from the ground up, but it bothered me that something this important was held together by toil and tribal knowledge. So I took on the 0→1 design of an internal Resilience Automation Platform on top of my existing drill responsibilities.

What drove me was simple: I wanted execution to survive without me hand-holding it. I owned the problem discovery, designed self-service scheduling so teams could run drills themselves, and built an internal AI agent with LLMs to automate planning. That meant a lot of late stretches reconciling how the system actually behaved against how teams wished it behaved, plus a high volume of partner conversations to get the workflows right.

The payoff was real. Operational toil dropped 30%, planning cycle time fell 39%, and drill capacity went up 21%, all while sustaining 45+ annual drills. More importantly, I turned a fragile process into a repeatable, scalable model partners could adopt on their own. That's the outcome I actually cared about.

## Why Ubiquiti specifically?

I platformized Azure's recovery validation across routing and network isolation to sustain 45+ annual drills, and I spent a lot of time translating distributed-systems behavior into narratives that both engineers and executives could act on. The idea that I'd help shape how UniFi evolves, not just how it's sold, is exactly the kind of influence I look for.
