## Describe a time when a customer asked you to configure a solution in a way that you knew was inefficient or unscalable. How did you guide them toward a better decision without damaging the relationship?

At Microsoft, early in building out Azure's resilience automation platform, one of the engineering stakeholders wanted to configure drill scheduling as a fully manual, one-off process - essentially a shared spreadsheet that a human would update before each drill. The team was comfortable with it because that's how things had always worked, and they didn't want to slow down an upcoming customer engagement to redesign it. I understood the instinct, but I also knew that approach would collapse under the volume we were targeting: 45+ drills a year across enterprise customers like Databricks, Walmart, and SAP.

Rather than pushing back directly, I framed it as a capacity question. I pulled together a quick breakdown of how many manual touchpoints the spreadsheet approach required per drill, multiplied it out across our projected annual volume, and showed the team where the bottlenecks would appear. Then I proposed a self-service scheduling workflow as a parallel workstream - something we could configure incrementally without blocking the immediate engagement. That reframe helped a lot. It wasn't 'your idea is wrong,' it was 'here's what happens at scale, and here's a path that gets us there without disrupting what you need right now.' The self-service workflow shipped and ended up reducing operational toil by 30% across all customer engagements. The relationship stayed strong because the team felt heard, not overruled.

## Walk us through a specific complex workflow or process you owned from discovery to configuration. How did you document the logic, and how did you validate that it actually met the business need?

The clearest example is Azure's first rack-level proactive resilience drill. There was no existing playbook for it - I started from scratch with discovery sessions across the engineering, operations, and customer teams to understand what a successful outcome actually looked like and what constraints we were working within. I captured the full workflow logic in a structured design doc: trigger conditions, dependency sequencing, expected recovery paths, and the specific failure scenarios we were testing for. Each decision point in the workflow had a documented rationale tied back to a stated customer requirement.

For configuration, I translated that design into a functional test structure with defined execution steps and checkpoints. Before we ran the drill live, I built a validation layer - essentially a dry-run script that walked through the logic and confirmed each step behaved as documented. That QA pass caught two sequencing issues that would have caused false negatives in the results. The live drill ran in four months from kickoff and achieved a 94% recovery rate. Post-drill, I updated the as-built documentation to reflect the final state of the configuration, including deviations from the original design and the rationale for each. That documentation became the foundation for subsequent drills and surfaced the hardware defects that drove $14M+ in business impact - which only happened because the validation process was disciplined enough to distinguish real failures from configuration noise.

## Tell us about a time during a project delivery when you realized a deadline was at risk due to a technical blocker or a change in scope. How did you communicate this to stakeholders?

During the legacy OS migration at Amazon Robotics, we were tracking a 2,000+ unit pilot across 1,200+ stations and had a hard deadline tied to operational continuity - zero downtime was non-negotiable. About midway through, we discovered that a subset of stations had undocumented hardware dependencies that weren't captured in the original migration map. Those dependencies meant the sequencing we had planned wouldn't hold for that cohort, and without a fix, we were looking at a realistic slip on that segment of the rollout.

I surfaced it immediately rather than waiting to see if the team could absorb it quietly. I put together a short written summary for the IT and Operations leads that laid out exactly what we found, which stations were affected, what the downstream risk was if we proceeded on the original timeline, and two options with tradeoffs - a targeted scope reduction that held the deadline for the unaffected stations, or a brief timeline extension with a mitigation plan for the affected cohort. I was specific about what I knew and honest about what was still uncertain. Leadership appreciated having options rather than just a problem. We went with the targeted approach: held the deadline for the bulk of the rollout and handled the affected stations as a follow-on sprint with a dedicated dependency review step built in. Zero operational downtime across the full pilot, and the dependency documentation we produced became part of the standard pre-migration checklist going forward.
