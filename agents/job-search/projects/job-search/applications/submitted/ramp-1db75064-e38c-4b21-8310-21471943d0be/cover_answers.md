## Please describe your experience with project management methodologies and practices.

Most of my TPM work has lived at the intersection of Agile execution and structured program management. At Amazon Robotics, I ran Agile ceremonies directly, including sprint planning and retrospectives, and owned the developer intake process by triaging and resolving 40+ high-priority tickets to keep cross-functional teams unblocked. That experience grounded me in how to keep execution velocity high when dependencies are messy and stakeholders span multiple orgs.

At Microsoft, the scale shifted but the fundamentals stayed the same. I owned programs end-to-end, from scoping and kickoff through delivery and retrospective. For the rack-level resilience drill program, I defined requirements, sequenced delivery, and established retrospective standards that the org now uses to learn from failures systematically. I also built playbooks to standardize what had previously been ad-hoc processes, which is how we scaled from a 2-person operation to 45+ annual drills without proportional headcount growth.

Across all of it, my approach has been pretty consistent: define clear ownership early, surface risk before it becomes a blocker, and make sure context flows between the technical teams doing the work and the stakeholders who need visibility. I lean on structured retrospectives not as a formality but as a real mechanism for raising the bar on execution over time.

## Please outline your experience translating a high-level roadmap into a set of discrete technical projects.

The clearest example of this is the Resilience Automation Platform I led at Microsoft. The high-level goal was straightforward: reduce operational toil and scale drill capacity without adding headcount. Translating that into actual work meant doing discovery first, mapping where manual effort was concentrated, identifying which parts of the planning and scheduling workflow were repeatable, and then scoping self-service capabilities in a sequence that delivered value incrementally rather than all at once. The end result was a 30% reduction in operational toil and a 21% increase in drill capacity, but getting there required breaking a broad mandate into concrete, ownable workstreams with clear acceptance criteria.

At Amazon Robotics, I did similar translation work on the OS migration. The high-level goal was migrating 2,000+ units without operational downtime. That required mapping dependencies across 1,200+ stations, identifying sequencing constraints, and building a rollout plan that protected production continuity at each phase. The roadmap was a starting point, but the actual work was decomposing it into discrete decisions: which stations move first, what the rollback criteria are, how IT, Operations, and Engineering stay aligned across handoffs.

More broadly, I think good roadmap-to-execution translation requires three things: enough technical depth to know what questions to ask engineers, enough stakeholder context to understand what constraints are real versus negotiable, and a bias toward writing things down so that the implicit logic of the roadmap becomes explicit and shared. That last part, turning fuzzy direction into documented scope and clear sequencing, is where I spend a lot of my energy.
