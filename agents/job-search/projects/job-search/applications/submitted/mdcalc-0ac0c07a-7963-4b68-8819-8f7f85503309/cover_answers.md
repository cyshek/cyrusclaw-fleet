## Describe a project where you had significant ownership in building a product, feature, or system. Be specific about what you built, the key decisions you made, your level of ownership, and the outcome.

At Microsoft I led the 0to1 development of an internal Resilience Automation Platform from scratch in a pretty ambiguous, fast-moving environment. The problem was that engineering teams were spending huge amounts of time on manual, repetitive resilience work, and there was no consistent way to do it. I owned this end to end, from problem definition and discovery through scoping, delivery, and post-launch measurement.

The key decisions came down to where to focus first. I dug into behavioral data and qualitative research to find the biggest engagement gaps, then made the call to build self-service flows that engineers could adopt without hand-holding, since adoption would die if the tool needed a lot of onboarding. I wrote the product requirements, user stories, and acceptance criteria that Engineering and Design built to, and I partnered with Data to instrument activation flows and define the success metrics we'd judge it against. I also pushed hard to design for return-habit usage, surfacing the right tools at the right moments so teams kept coming back rather than using it once.

The outcome was a platform that reduced operational toil by 30% and built recurring usage across engineering teams. On a related effort I shipped Azure's first proactive resilience testing capability, a rack-level program delivered in 4 months with a 94% recovery rate. Across the broader portfolio this work sustained 45+ annual drills and drove $14M+ in business impact. What I took away from it is that owning a product means owning the outcome, not just shipping the requirements, and that's the part I care most about.
