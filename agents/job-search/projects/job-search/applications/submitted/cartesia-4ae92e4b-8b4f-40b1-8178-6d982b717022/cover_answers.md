## Why Cartesia?

At Microsoft I got to operate as the CEO of a 0→1 initiative, taking Azure's recovery validation from a 2-person effort into a platformized product that sustained 45+ annual resilience drills and drove $14M+ in business impact across Databricks, Walmart, SAP, and NetApp. I owned the roadmap, ran discovery with customers personally, sparred with engineering on architecture and eval design, and pushed resilience commitments to close alongside GTM and field teams. That full loop of discovery, building, and selling on top of foundation systems is exactly what this role is asking for, and it's the work I find most energizing.

Cartesia appeals to me because you're building on a genuinely novel primitive. State Space Models out of the Stanford AI Lab are not an incremental bet, they're a different way to train efficient large-scale foundation models, and applying that to real-time and applied AI is a rare chance to define a product category early. I want to be the person who turns that research edge into something customers can't live without, set the pace for a squad of researchers and engineers, and become a credible external voice for the work. The combination of deep model innovation and a ship-fast culture is the environment where I do my best work.

## What's the hardest problem you've worked on?

The hardest problem I've worked on was pioneering Azure's first proactive resilience testing capability. Before this, the org validated recovery reactively and at small scale, and there was no precedent for deliberately testing failure on live production infrastructure. I had to build conviction across research, engineering, and partner teams that this was worth doing, then actually ship a rack-level drill program in 4 months that hit a 94% recovery rate and surfaced critical hardware defects that would otherwise have stayed hidden.

What made it genuinely hard was the ambiguity and the stakes. I was running 14 executive-visible recovery executions, including a sovereign-cloud isolation test tied to a $1.5B+ enterprise contract, where a mistake had real consequences. I had no formal authority over the teams I needed, so I treated every blocker as a personal problem to solve and used primers, narratives, and POVs to align people who didn't report to me. I also built an internal AI agent that restructured the planning workflow, which cut planning cycle time 39% and increased drill capacity 21%, because the manual process couldn't keep up with the pace we needed.

The lesson I took from it is that the technical work was only half the battle. The harder part was translating messy customer and production evidence into opinionated bets, killing the work that wasn't worth doing, and getting a cross-functional group to move fast together without anyone telling them they had to. That's the muscle I want to keep building.
