# Part 1 — Wyatt Roderick: "AI Arbitrage Agency"

## The business model in one paragraph

You sell local service businesses (HVAC, dental, real estate, solar, roofing, etc.) a **90-day package for $10,000 upfront** that delivers (1) a Facebook/Meta lead-generation system, (2) an AI voice agent that auto-calls and books appointments with those leads, and (3) a refreshed sales script. **You personally fulfill none of this** — you stack a white-label ads agency under you, hire an Upwork dev to build the AI caller, and use a VA for script work. Your job is sales + management. You can optionally tack on a **$1-2k/month retainer** plus a revenue-share commission on closed deals the client gets from your system.

## The unit economics he shows on screen

Per $10k deal:
| Line item | Cost | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| White-label ads agency (Stealth Agency, Hydra, etc.) | $500/mo × 3 = $1,500 | Client pays ad-spend separately; this is fulfillment fee |
| Upwork AI voice dev (retell.ai or Synthflow) | $1,000 flat | One-time per deal; same dev across all clients |
| Shared VA for script work | ~$300/mo, amortized | Spread across clients |
| **Your gross margin per deal** | **~$7,200 (~72%)** | Recurring upsells layered on top |

He frames it as: "$97 to start (GoHighLevel + Stripe + a domain), 70–80% margin, recurring, and demand is genuinely surging because every local business hears 'AI' in the news and wants in."

## Niche selection (he ranks them)

- **Easy:** Roofers, HVAC, pool, solar, landscaping, pest control. Home services. Reasoning: high ticket per customer ($5k–50k jobs), already buy ads, already understand "leads."
- **Medium:** Bookkeepers, real estate agents, mortgage brokers, accountants, small law firms.
- **Hard:** Other agencies, consultants, info-product sellers, coaches. These people see the trick.
- **Hyperniching is mandatory.** "`brush.ai` for dentists" out-converts `100kaiagency.com` every time. Generalist agencies die.

## Client acquisition (he explicitly rejects cold outreach)

Two channels, in order of preference:

**1. Paid Meta ads.** Tools he names:
- `arcads.ai` — AI-actor video creatives (you don't film yourself).
- Canva magic-expand on ChatGPT-generated images for static ads.
- Claude AI for scripts, prompted with `"write like Dan Henry"` (a direct-response copywriter).
- Direct opt-in funnel; client books a call from the ad.

**2. YouTube content.** His approach:
- Install the **VidIQ** browser extension.
- Find videos on competitor channels that performed **N× better than that channel's average** (VidIQ surfaces this).
- Copy the title + thumbnail style + structure (not the content). The market has already proven this hook converts.
- "Even a 10-view video works — that's potentially one HVAC owner Googling for help. One client = $10k."

## The funnel he runs (GoHighLevel under the hood)

```
Ad / video  →  Opt-in page  →  Calendar page  →  Pre-call page (video + testimonials)
                                                          ↓
              [reminder chain: SMS @ 6min, 1h, 24h, 48h; AI voice calls weekly forever;
               14-day Claude-written email sequence titled "buy, die, or say goodbye"]
                                                          ↓
              Post-booking: 7-step reminder chain ending with a pre-recorded voicemail
              drop 10 minutes before the call
                                                          ↓
                                                    Sales call
```

## The sales call (1-hour breakdown, this is his most repeated content)

**Opening / pre-call check:**
- Verify they actually watched the pre-call video. If not → REBOOK. Hard rule. He says calls where the video wasn't watched close at <10%.

**Discovery — "killing the zombies":**
- Pre-emptively surface every objection so they don't haunt the close: "Walk me through your last attempt at marketing. What worked, what didn't?" → digs until you've pre-handled budget, timing, trust, and 'I need to think.'

**Pitch (30–60 seconds, on a Lucid Chart, never live systems):**
- Three pillars, hand-drawn boxes:
  1. We get you leads.
  2. AI calls them so you never miss one.
  3. We give your team a script so they actually close.
- "Confused buyers don't buy. Never pull up live software during the pitch."

**Conversion / close:**
- The line he repeats most: *"Man-to-man, I take 6-7 of these calls a day. People tell me three things — heck yeah, screw you, or 'I gotta think about it.' Can we agree to keep it a simple yes or no today?"*
- This pre-empties the #1 stall objection.

**Objection utility belt (visible in the call):**
- Custom AI ROI calculator he built himself (lead per niche × close rate × ticket × your fee).
- McKinsey AI productivity data (he openly says: "steal it, present it as your own research").
- Trustpilot reviews tab pre-loaded.

## Video catalog signal — what he covers in his 13 most-viewed videos

All 13 are variations on this same offer, repackaged with different headline angles:
- **Long-form courses:** Complete AI Agency Sales Course (2+ hrs), Free 1-Hour AI Arbitrage Course.

The pattern itself is part of the lesson: **one offer, dozens of headline variants, ride whichever VidIQ says is winning.**

## What's specifically different about his version vs. older "SMMA" gurus

- Adds the AI voice caller layer (retell.ai / Synthflow) as the differentiator from generic SMMA.
- Uses Claude/ChatGPT heavily in the back office (scripts, emails, ROI calculators).
- Specifically arbitrages the gap between "small business owner saw AI on Fox News" and "actual AI implementation cost is $1k on Upwork."

# Part 2 — subi: TikTok-Shop-Arbitrage Dropshipping (post-Valorant era)

## The business model in one paragraph

Find products selling well on **TikTok Shop** using a tool called **Kalodata**, **rip the UGC video ads** that creators are already running for those products, **port them to Meta** (Facebook/Instagram) where the same product isn't yet saturated, run **Advantage+ AI campaigns at $100-150/day** to test, and scale the winners. Source from AliExpress initially, switch to **1688.com** (the wholesale layer underneath AliExpress) once you have volume, ship from China direct to customer. He claims he did $1.8M as a full-time student running this.

## Product selection (his 6 criteria)

A product is testable if it has all of:
1. **Solves a deep emotional desire** — insecurity, convenience, or pain. Not "cool gadget."
2. **Visually self-explanatory** — the ad sells it in 3 seconds with no voiceover required.
3. **Lightweight and small** — <60cm and <5kg. China shipping math breaks above that.
4. **Already has UGC** existing on TikTok Shop. (Validation: someone else is profitably running it.)
5. **New to market** — roughly 1 month old, not 1 year. Older = saturated; your ads will lose the Meta auction to incumbents with better learned-pixels.
6. **Seasonal counts.** Always check Google Trends 5-year view to confirm you're entering on the upswing, not the way down.

## Pricing math

- **3× true cost (from 1688.com) + shipping + $1-2 service fee = minimum sell price.** 4-5× ideal.
- He explicitly prefers **higher-AOV products**: a $30 cost item sold at $90 with a $60 gross gives you ad-spend headroom. A $10 cost sold at $30 with a $20 gross usually loses money once Meta takes its cut.

## Store building (deliberately minimalist)

He shows Hotjar-style heatmaps demonstrating **60-80% of buyers never scroll past the hero section.** So:
- Build only the hero section well: hero image, USP bullets, visual offer block (he uses the **Kaching** Shopify app), guarantees ("free shipping," "30-day returns"), and an FAQ.
- "Site should take 20-30 minutes."
- **Steal competitor product photos initially.** Worry about original assets after you've validated demand.
- Don't waste time on homepage; nobody lands there.

## Ad strategy — the most differentiated part of his playbook

**Creative sourcing:**
- Pull 6-10 UGC videos from Kalodata for your chosen product.
- **Always cross-check Facebook Ad Library** — if a competitor is already running the same creative on Meta, you'll lose the auction every single time. Drop it.

**Account warmup:**
- Brand-new Meta ad account = bot-flag risk. He warms it up by running **$3-4 of engagement spend** on a picture of his dog. "Just to prove you're a human, then run the actual product."

**Campaign setup:**
- US-only targeting.
- **CBO** (Campaign Budget Optimization), not ABO.
- **Advantage+ AI placements.** Let Meta decide where ads run.
- **One ad per ad-set.** Don't muddy the learning signal.
- **Don't touch anything for 3 days.** No bid changes, no creative swaps. Meta needs the learning phase.

## The 3-day product test (his core metric framework)

| Day | Target CPM | Target CPC | Target ATC% | Required Sales |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | <$50 | <$2 | **5-10%** (read from Shopify, not Meta) | None |
| 2 | dropping | — | trending up | 1+ ideal |
| 3 | $30-40 | — | — | break-even or profit |

**Add-to-Cart % is the leading indicator he cares about most.** "Sales lag, ATC doesn't. Read it from Shopify, not Meta — Meta misattributes."

**Cut on day 3** if metrics aren't trending the right direction across all of: CPM, CPC, ATC, ROAS. Don't try to save a loser.

## After the test — three outcome buckets per ad-set

1. **High spend, no sales** → concept is fine, execution is bad. Rebuild script using the same concept (same hook angle, new copy/footage).
2. **High spend + sales** → reiterate variations of the *winning* concept (more of the same hook, more angles around it).
3. **Low spend + sales** → leave it alone. "Force-spending will kill it." Meta is intentionally rationing impressions because it's working — let the AI scale it organically.

## Scaling (day 4+)

- **New angles, not new budgets.** Same product, but new audience angles: dog-owners → parents → relationships → fitness.
- **New formats**: UGC testimonial → voiceover → side-by-side comparison → trend hijack.
- **Creative fatigue is real and fast.** You need a pipeline of new concepts ready, not budget pumps.
- **One testing campaign per store.** New ad-set per new ad. Cleaner data.

## Specialization videos covered

- **Seasonal dropshipping (Q4 playbook):** Q4 is the "cheat code." Sell products where demand already exists with no convincing — fleece tights, heated jackets, cooling blankets going into the right season. Scale aggressively on ~day 12 when the seasonal trend graph goes exponential.
- **AI / non-human creators:** Teases using AI-generated UGC actors (likely arcads.ai workflow, same as Wyatt). Lowers creative production cost dramatically.
- **40k dropshippers surveyed:** The top reported struggles, in order, are: picking products, trusting suppliers, ad fatigue. He uses this as social proof to recruit into his coaching, but the data itself is useful.
- **"Beliefs that keep you broke":** Mindset/sales-page content for his coaching program.

|---|---|---|
| Kalodata | Find winning TikTok Shop products + rip UGC | ~$100/mo |
| Facebook Ad Library | Free competitor-creative check | Free |
| Shopify | Storefront | $29/mo + Kaching app |

# Part 3 — Cross-channel pattern: identical YouTube funnel mechanics

Both creators use the same content engine. Worth understanding even if you never sell info-products yourself, because **the mechanics work for any high-ticket offer.**

```
YouTube content (free, long-form)
    ↓
Opt-in form (one URL in description, repeated verbally on screen)
    ↓
Calendar booking
    ↓
Pre-call nurture (video + testimonials + reminders)
    ↓
Sales call (the actual conversion)
    ↓
Coaching / done-for-you offer ($1k–$25k typical range)
```

**Shared headline patterns** (steal these for any niche):
- "$0 to $XX,XXX in N days"
- "How I make $X/month doing Y"
- "Free X-hour Z course"
- "Stop doing X, do Y instead"
- "I asked N people their biggest struggle"

**Shared authority moves:**
- Live dashboard footage, even just a few seconds (Stripe, Shopify, Meta Ads Manager).
- Age-based hook ("I'm 17/18/19") when applicable — implies the system is so good even a kid runs it.
- Specifically naming tools mid-video (Kalodata, retell.ai, GoHighLevel) — proves you actually use them.
- 1 long-form anchor video per month + 4-8 case-study reskins of the same offer.

**Shared self-improvement loop:**
- They use VidIQ on themselves: titles, thumbnails, and intros A/B-tested via VidIQ's "outlier" metric (this video did 3× the channel's median).

# Part 4 — Reusable assets, regardless of whether you ever copy either business

These are pure mechanics that apply to literally any offer (product, service, course, SaaS):

1. **Pre-call nurture video.** Slideshow + voiceover, 3-7 minutes, frontloads your value and pre-handles objections. Massively reduces close-call friction. Wyatt's hard rule of "REBOOK if not watched" is harsh but probably correct.
2. **14-day Claude-written email sequence** ("write like Dan Henry"). Universal for any list you ever build. Wyatt's "buy, die, or say goodbye" frame is just plain good email-sequence design.
3. **"Killing the zombies" objection pre-handling** during discovery. Universal sales-call structure improvement.
4. **Lucid Chart 3-pillar pitch in 30-60 seconds.** "Don't pull up live systems" is correct for almost any complex offer — the pitch is the simple version, the system is the delivery.
5. **7-step post-booking reminder chain.** Increases show-up rates dramatically for any sales call.
6. **VidIQ "outlier" methodology** for picking content topics — applies to any YouTube channel anyone ever builds.
7. **AI ROI calculator as a lead magnet** — Wyatt's calculator is genuinely useful. The pattern of "give the prospect a number, then sell them the means to capture that number" works in many B2B contexts.
8. **subi's 3-day ad test framework.** Even if you never dropship, the metric thresholds (CPM/CPC/ATC%, day-3 cut rule) are how you'd evaluate any paid-acquisition channel for any product.
9. **Kalodata → Meta Ad Library cross-check** is a generalizable creative-research workflow: find what's working on Platform A but not yet on Platform B, port it.

# Part 5 — My personal take (clearly labeled, skip if you want)

> Reminder: this is **my** opinion. The substance above is what they actually teach; the substance is what you should base your judgment on. I'm adding this because you asked for honest assessment, not because my take should override your read.

**On Wyatt's AI agency business specifically:**
- The niche is now extremely crowded — 1000+ creators teaching some version of "AI agency for local biz" in late 2025 / early 2026. The marginal new entrant has to fight a saturated information ecosystem on the prospect side (every HVAC owner has been pitched this 5 times).
- It is also somewhat structurally awkward for you specifically: you build the AI agent layer (OpenClaw, this whole fleet). Selling Upwork-built retell.ai voice bots to roofers puts you one layer below your own existing tech. Wyatt's playbook is more interesting to you as **sales/funnel intel** than as a business to copy verbatim.
- That said, the **mechanics are real.** Calls do convert at the rates he shows when the funnel is run tightly. The economics do work at $10k tickets for someone with sales chops and patience.

**On subi's dropshipping business specifically:**
- The method is real cashflow. People genuinely do net 10-20% margins running this disciplined version. The Q4 seasonal window is the lowest-risk entry point.
- Capital requirement is real: ~$3-5k of ad budget you have to be willing to lose during testing before you find a winner. The "3-day cut rule" is what protects you from losing more than that, but you still need the cushion.
- Time requirement is real: 30-50 hr/week during active testing/scaling phases. Not passive. Not background.
- Doesn't conflict with trading-bench (different game).
- subi's revenue claims ("$0 to $115k in 10 days," "$1.8M as a student") are **revenue, not profit.** He never breaks out margins explicitly. At his own stated 3× markup with ads at 50-70% of revenue, net margin is likely 10-20%. So $1.8M revenue ≈ $180-360k net, which is still a great outcome but it's a different number.

**On both as YouTube creators:**
- Both are openly running coaching funnels. The free videos are the top of that funnel. This is the most common business model on educational YouTube and isn't inherently bad — they teach real mechanics and people do execute successfully on the free content alone.
- Both also dunk on competitors' models specifically to position their own, which is normal salesperson behavior — worth filtering for when you're reading either of them.
