## 0) The Big Picture — Carson's Acquisition Philosophy

Carson runs a ~**$2.5M/yr AI agency** (his words) in the **stem-cell / peptide medical clinic niche**. His client-acquisition stack, in priority order:

1. **Paid ads (primary, "predictable acquisition system")** — books **8–10+ sales calls/day**. This is his real engine.
2. **Outreach (DM/LinkedIn/cold email)** — what he used to land his *first* clients with $0; he now barely does it but teaches it for broke beginners.
3. **Organic content (YouTube/IG)** — long-term snowball; nurtures inbound + warms booked calls.

His core thesis, stated bluntly across videos:

> "A lot of people give bad information online on how you should book sales calls… you should sit around and do outreach and cold calling all day… Obviously, that is the most inefficient way to scale."

> "Most people on YouTube are going to tell you to go to outreach… you go to Google Maps… and you cold call them… Is that a joke? Are we done with this? … no one does that. That is not predictable any way, shape, or form. What is predictable is running paid ads."

> "You trade your money for time… you give Facebook money so that you can book appointments essentially while you're sleeping."

**The duplication game (his mental model for the whole business):**
> "You essentially spend $1,000 to acquire a client that pays you, let's say, three grand. So you're making a 3x return. And you use that three grand… to go fuel your next three clients, your next four clients, five clients. And it's like a duplication game. That's… how I've gotten so big over the years."

## 1) OUTREACH SYSTEM (the free, beginner channel)

Source: `L1LsEN_iuIc` (Carson + Marcus, 7-yr outreach specialist) and `8F_P8gf5Pbc` (Carson's own first-client story).

### 1a. When to use outreach at all
Carson is blunt — outreach is the **fallback for the broke**:
> "The only time you do outreach, and I say to start here just if you have no money… If you have $0, you're 13 years old, and you're in a third-world country… outreach is the move. But if you… live in a country where you can go make minimum wage, $15 an hour… [paid ads] is the move. And if that scares you, bite the bullet."

He literally tells people without a $1,500 ad budget: **"You need to get a job."**

But he still credits outreach for his start:
> "I closed this first ever client through just a regular outreach method. I think it was a cold email. And again, this is after like 7 months of trying cold calls, every single method possible…"

### 1b. The five fundamentals (Marcus, "the foundation of everything")
1. **Offer is 80% of the game.** "80% of success again comes from a good offer… not… 'oh, we offer meta ads, we offer SEO.' …what you do need to share is tangible proven results… It could be more qualified appointments. …make that tangible. How many could you get them within 30 days, within the first 7 days?" (He name-drops Alex Hormozi's *$100M Offers* — "follow that to a tea.")
2. **Volume negates luck.** "We want to do 50 even to 100 DMs a day." Carson's reinforcement: "if you do five DMs in one day compared to the guy who does 100 DMs… the guy with 100 DMs is working 20x what you are… so he's 20 times more likely to close."
3. **Consistency is non-negotiable.** "It's gonna be boring at first… after doing two, three weeks of this… you're going to see how powerful this is."
4. **Follow-up is necessary** — "It literally doubles the amount of appointments you get."
5. **Fish where your niche swims.** "Roofers are obviously not on LinkedIn… where they are active are on Facebook in certain Facebook groups."

### 1c. Channel → niche matching
- **LinkedIn** = white-collar/B2B: lawyers, real estate agents, mortgage brokers, financial services (CPAs, planners), solar, insurance, consultants, private equity.
- **Facebook + Instagram** = blue-collar + local: landscapers, roofers, HVAC, flooring, "anything around the house," med spas, gyms, real estate agents.
- **Facebook groups** are the single best Facebook tactic: "going into a private group and then messaging people within that group… they will see that commonality… you guys are both in the same group."

### 1d. THE PRE-WARMING PROTOCOL (do this BEFORE any DM)
This is the most important mechanical lesson. Cold DMs are dead:
> "Gone are the days where you can just send a DM right there and they'll respond back. Instagram doesn't like it. LinkedIn doesn't like it. The prospect doesn't like it. So don't even do it."

The protocol:
1. Follow the prospect first (IG prioritizes DMs between followers).
2. Engage **3–5 of their recent posts spread over a couple of days**.
3. Leave **one genuine specific comment** ("a quick congratulatory comment on their grand opening… or a new machine they brought into their med spa").
4. THEN DM. Result: **"This pre-warming step improves delivery rates by 40–50%."**
5. Also post **1–2 stories/day during ramp-up** to build account trust score (like an email warm-up).

### 1e. THE 3-PART OPENING MESSAGE (verbatim framework)
Golden rule first: **"You're opening a conversation… not closing a sale… You're not pitch slapping people."** ~2–3 sentences max, no price, no full offer.

- **Part 1 — Hook (personalized opener):**
  > "Saw your recent post about your opening, your grand opening in Anaheim, California. Congratulations."
- **Part 2 — Bridge (relevant + transparent about why you're messaging):**
  > "Yeah, I work with another self-made gym facility in Long Beach on their lead generation via Meta. Curious where you're getting most of your new clients for your grand opening right now in Anaheim."
- **Alt bridge (med spa example):**
  > "Hey, love that post you made. One of my med spa clients, we're helping them sell more M sculpting. I was curious, are you selling that or is it collecting dust in the corner of your studio?"

Marcus's warning on being too vague (the bait-and-switch trap):
> "What everyone does in the DMs is they try to be too vague… and the ultimate outcome… when you finally do pitch them is you just 180 them into a pitch slap and they just feel like, 'Oh okay, I didn't see that coming.' …that's where they start to ignore you."

### 1f. LinkedIn connection-request scripts
LinkedIn is a two-step system (connect → then message). Keep the connect note generic:
> "Hey, saw our positions overlapped / saw we're in the same industry, thought it would be nice to connect."
> "Hey, saw you're an outdoor enthusiast just like me. I thought it would be nice to connect. I was also curious if you're focused on your marketing with Meta and how that's changing with AI."

Because LinkedIn is B2B, you can even state intent directly:
> "Hey, curious if you're focused on getting more clients right now for your gym. Wondering if it'd be a good fit for a partnership."

### 1g. FOLLOW-UP SEQUENCE (verbatim — "doubles appointments")
Reality check: "8 times out of 10, 9 times out of 10, prospects are not going to book that same day." Cadence = **Mon → Tue → Thu → Sat/Mon** (≈ full 7 days, 3–4 touches):

1. **Follow-up 1 (next day):** "Know you're swamped with work, curious if [landing more roofing jobs] is a focus of yours right now."
2. **Follow-up 2 (+2 days):** "We're helping other roofers achieve [desired outcome]. Figured you'd want to know what's working." *(insert a case study here if you have one)*
3. **Follow-up 3 (final, ~7–10 days):** "Hey last quick check, going to assume you're good with [blank], doors are always open if that changes." — OR the line Marcus says reactivates the most gym owners:
   > "Hey, seems like you're super duper busy with your current clients. I'm going to put the conversation on the shelf for now here if looking for more clients is an absolute priority."
   > → "A lot of them said 'sorry, I've been busy, what's up?'"

### 1h. Volume / safety limits (2026, per Marcus)
- **Instagram/Facebook manual:** 10–20 msgs morning + another batch afternoon, per account. **No automation** on IG/FB ("they detect it really fast").
- **LinkedIn:** hard limit **100 invites/week** standard, **200 with Sales Navigator Core**. Safe sustainable = **80–120/week**. Spread **15–20/day** (never blast all on Monday — "signals automation").
- **Sales Navigator Core is a must for serious LinkedIn** (unlocks InMails to bypass connection requests → "an extra 20 conversations a month and ultimately 5 extra booked appointments").
- **KPI to watch:** acceptance rate. "An acceptance rate below 30% triggers LinkedIn to tighten your invite ceiling on its own."
- **Automation tools (LinkedIn only, account >1yr old):** Prospeo.ai-type, "I Alfred" (Aimfox), plus Marcus's own. **Never automate the reply** — "they will test you… with 'what's the catch, what's the cost' and you do not want to automate those."
- **Buying aged accounts:** mentions bulkaccountsby.com (mixed feelings — "honestly I would actually still make your own accounts").
- **Organization:** simple Google Sheet pipeline (prospect, notes, stage, follow-up dates color-coded). "If you don't track your follow-ups… then don't expect to close clients."
- **Don't delegate to a VA until YOU win:** "If you're not seeing 2, 3 or so appointments minimum a week, what's a VA going to do? …It's never going to be your VA's fault. You're only as good as you train them."

### 1i. Carson's preferred beginner outreach OFFER (risk reversal baked in)
> "I would number one do outreach with a pay-per-result sort of model in mind… 'we will get you new jobs on a pay-per-job basis. You only pay me when we close a job.' …it's very hard for people to say no to that because it sounds like there's no risk… And realistically I would take the free trials… that is 100% true. …I would start with free trials or the $300 for 3-week stuff like I did."

## 2) THE PAID-ADS BOOKING ENGINE (how the calls actually get on the calendar)

Source: `vL07X7Dhr-Q`. This is how Carson *really* books 8–10 calls/day. Summarized — it's the lead-gen that everything else depends on.

- **Platform:** Meta (Facebook/Instagram), objective = **Leads → conversion location = Website** (not Meta lead forms). "You want the leads conversion. You don't want anything else."
- **Structure:** He prefers **ABO** (ad-set budget optimization) over CBO for clean testing data. Turn OFF "share budget with other ad sets."
- **3 ad-set targeting types under one campaign:** Broad / Interest stack (≤5 interests) / Lookalike (his best performer; built from a scraped lead list uploaded as a custom audience, 0–1% & 1–2%).
- **Budget = a "gambling budget you're willing to lose."** Recommends **$1,500–$2,000 to land the first client**; he personally spends **$1,500–$2,000/day** ($45–60K/mo). Medical niche cost-per-call is **$100+**; roofing/real estate ≈ **$30–50/call**.
- **Creative rule of thumb (post-Andromeda update):** **5 ads per $50/day of spend**, scaling up to a max of **25 active ads per ad set**. Made fast with **Ideogram (images)** and **Arc Ads (AI video)** + ChatGPT/Claude for copy. Ad copy structure = **Hook (who) → Body (offer) → CTA (Learn More)**.
- **The funnel** (all in GoHighLevel): Ad → landing page w/ calendar → **booking** → **pre-call page** (3–5 min expectation-setting video + "breakout" objection-handling videos) → thank-you page. Pixel fires `PageView` on landing, `Schedule` on thank-you page (the optimization event).
- **Pre-call page = pre-selling.** Sets tone, demands a quiet environment + pen/notepad, threatens to "blacklist" no-shows, and pre-handles objections with bite-size videos: "How does the program work? / How much does this cost? / I've been burned before, how do I know this isn't a scam?"
- **Tracking edge:** UTM **URL parameters** on every ad so GoHighLevel shows exactly which ad/ad-set/campaign each closed deal came from. "You can track exactly what ad they came from… rather than what Facebook and what you assume is making you money."
- **Adding friction to raise quality** (only when you have volume): qualifying question on the calendar (revenue), "why do you need help," then a VSL. "Add friction when you see volume but lower quality. Don't do it vice versa."
- **"Hammer them" nurture** (Jeremy Haynes-inspired): a separate $10–20/day retargeting campaign of 10–15 education ads hitting only people who booked in the last 7 days, frequency 5–10×, cycling them through all videos before the call.

**Takeaway:** the *quality of the sales conversation* is heavily front-loaded by this machine — by call time the prospect is pre-educated, pre-qualified (and on the live call, ideally has a deposit down).

## 3) THE SALES-CALL FRAMEWORK (the universal structure)

Source: `9TRPg0Rj1i8` (the pitch he literally screen-shares) + `9GJpRIqYz_4` (live execution).

Carson sells a **3-pillar AI program**, drawn live on a Lucidchart the prospect copies onto pen & paper. He says verbatim:
> "Present your three-pillar AI program exactly like how I do in my sales calls."

**Have the prospect write down 3 things** (the closer does this on the live call):
1. **Lead Generation** — AI-powered Meta ads (40–50 new ads/month so you "actually crack the advertisement code").
2. **Appointment Setting** — US-based setters call the lead **within 60–120 seconds** of opt-in + an AI caller / nurture system; **deposits / "consultation fee" collected** to kill tire-kickers.
3. **Sales / Closing** — either provide a closer, or rebuild their sales script; framed as "the AI caller is something you **keep** beyond working with us."

**Why this framing works (Carson's words):**
> "Business owners do not care how you built the AI… They care about more leads, more booked appointments, and more revenue that you are actively driving to their business that they can track."

**The keep-it-forever anchor** (justifies the big upfront):
> "The big thing is the AI caller, and the AI caller is something you keep beyond even working with us… for the AI caller, we can essentially build this and implement it directly into their business, and they keep it… And that's why we charge big amounts."

### Universal call sequence Carson/Jacob run:
1. **~20 seconds of small talk**, then straight into discovery. (On the live call: "I went from like 20 seconds of small talk to straight into pretty much establishing…")
2. **Discovery / differentiation** — surface current pain, and *contrast your process against their current agency* in real time ("they do that, they do this, why are you doing that?").
3. **Quantify the gap** — get them to state their revenue goal and current numbers, then do the math live so the cost of inaction is obvious.
4. **Drop price EARLY** (before the presentation — see §5).
5. **Walk the 3 pillars**, weaving education + their specific pains throughout.
6. **Handle objections** (money, communication, trust — see §4).
7. **Close** / arrange financing / take deposit.

## 4) THE $15,000 DIFFICULT LIVE CALL — Turn-by-Turn

Source: `9GJpRIqYz_4`. Prospect = owner of a new **stem-cell clinic** (soft-opened Oct 15). Closer = **Jacob**; a setter/Ethan pre-qualified. Carson narrates over it. **This is the most instructive artifact in the batch.**

### What made it "difficult"
- Prospect had been **burned by two prior agencies** (one called "Business Health AI" that "promised seven, eight figures").
- Clinic is **losing money**: ~**$12K/mo revenue vs $16K/mo overhead** ("code red," per Carson). He'd already **laid off staff** and **postponed a 2nd clinic** (DC).
- 100+ leads/mo but only ~6 reach the clinic → **bad leads ("tire kickers, Medicare moms") and bad follow-up**.
- The killer: **the prospect couldn't actually afford the $15K upfront** ("honestly I'm making my mortgage payment… I can't take it").

### The actual flow

**1. Open (rapport, ~20s).** Jacob: *"How's it going?"* → quick banter about 4 hours of sleep, then pivots fast: *"Ethan gave me a little recap… you have your stem cell clinic you opened back in like October, correct?"*

**2. Discovery via the prospect's own words.** Prospect volunteers the pain: bad leads, no deposits, prior agency "refuses" to charge a booking fee. Jacob immediately weaponizes it:
- *"You're not doing that right now."* (charging a deposit)
- Carson's voiceover: "He's essentially completely differentiating what we do and what this client currently works with… they aren't getting deposits."

**3. Pain-funnel question (makes prospect say the cost out loud).** When the prospect explains he has to educate patients *after* they arrive, Jacob runs a mini-Socratic close:
> Jacob: "But what happens if they're not educated before they come in? You're burning a lot of time…"
> Jacob: "Not just time. What else are you burning, man?"
> Prospect: "Well, money."
> Jacob: "There you go. Right."

**4. Quantify the goal + do the math live.** Jacob pins the numbers:
- Prospect's realistic goal: "**4 or 5 clients a week**." Avg ED treatment = **$5,300** (profit **$3,500**). Jacob: *"So that's roughly 15 to 18,000 off of 5 to 6 patients."* → which is **more in one week than the clinic currently does in a month**. Carson: "He should be able to create a lot of urgency here just due to this guy being in such a rough position."
- The trial-close that locks the goal: *"With those small changes, do you think you could reach that easily?"* → Prospect: *"Yes, I know I could. I wouldn't be wasting my time with this if I didn't think so."*

**5. Price drop — BEFORE the pitch (the signature move).**
> Jacob: "Three things I want you to write down… lead generation… appointment setting… and sales. …So right away, I'm not going to hide the price from you. Price is for the full 90 days, it's 15,000. But what you're going to get for that is…"

Carson's explanation (see §5 for full quote): they always drop price up front to clear the prospect's brain.

**6. Walk the 3 pillars, weaponizing his stated pains.**
- **Pillar 1 (Lead gen):** "We launch 40 to 50 new advertisements every single month… because even two or three words can change your conversion… by 10, 15, 20%." Humans script, AI does supplementary visuals. Emphasizes **361 compliance** (medical) as a differentiator vs templatized agencies.
- **Pillar 2 (Appointment setting):** US-based setters call **within 60–120 seconds**, run a 15–20 min structured call, qualify on financial capability + medical background, **collect a non-refundable "consultation fee" that rolls into treatment**. Jacob explicitly contrasts this with the prospect's current agency:
  > "What we do is we also do deposits. So [the lead pays] a consultation fee and it's non-refundable. However, what we do is [roll it into treatment]… so they're not no-showing… with a consultation fee, nobody who [isn't serious] is going to pay that consultation fee. Make sense?"
  > Carson voiceover: the deposit is the single biggest lever against the "tire kickers / Medicare moms" problem — "they aren't getting deposits" with the old agency, which is *why* only 6 of 100 leads showed.
- **Pillar 3 (Sales/closing + reporting):** a dedicated account manager (named "Skyler" on the call) sends **weekly 20–25 min screen-recorded stat updates** + **weekly 1-on-1 calls**. This is offered specifically to answer the prospect's communication worry.

**7. Objection sequence (the hard part — verbatim).** Three real objections fired, each handled by reframe, not discount:

- **Objection: "Communication — you've got lots of customers, we don't expect you to drop everything."**
  > Jacob: "Weekly statistic updates. So Skyler would do that. He'd go through the ad account… send you guys a 20, 25 minute video… and you get those weekly calls with Skyler one-on-one."
  > → Resolved by *productizing* the worry into a named deliverable.

- **Objection (THE big one): "I don't know if I can scrape up the 15… honestly I'm making my mortgage payment, I'm taking everything else… I can't take it."**
  Jacob's response is a textbook 3-step: (a) validate, (b) isolate, (c) commitment reframe.
  > (a) Validate: "I respect that move the ones and zeros. Whatever needs to be done, I can respect that situation. I've done it."
  > (b) **Isolation question:** "My question is, if money were out of the equation, would you do this?"
  > Prospect: "Yeah, absolutely. There's no question… you and I would be partners today. No one else is talking to me like this." *(Carson voiceover: "that is the key part… you have to speak their lingo… pick the niche you already have the background with.")*
  > (c) **Money-as-symptom reframe:** "Do you mind if I share a little bit of perspective around the money? Most of our successful clients, they don't view money as a problem, they view it as a symptom. Because if you had the right marketing… the right team bringing you qualified leads with deposits down… what else would you have more of?" → Prospect: "more money."
  > **The commitment line:** "I can help with the investment. But I can't help with the commitment itself. That commitment has to come from you. Whether it be today, tomorrow, doesn't really matter. But being willing… and not letting that uncertainty overcome what you want to do — are you willing to do that?" → Prospect: "I am willing to do that."

**8. The close = financing pivot (NOT a discount).** Once commitment was verbal but cash was the only blocker, Jacob broke the $15K into payments rather than cutting price:
  > Jacob: "If we could break that down and make it easier for you, would that be something you're willing to do?"
  > Final structure offered: **$1,300/month** (financed) **+ $3,000/mo ad spend** — and the anchor: *"which is still less than what you're just paying on ad spend with this other company."* Plus a path to *"just pay the whole thing off in March when we get to close [on his other business]."*
  > Risk-sharing frame: "We've all got skin in this game, right? My success is your success long term."

**9. Outcome (Carson's TL;DR):** "Scheduled the [onboarding] call same day. **Got a deposit** and still to this day we're waiting to hear back… he's just paid a deposit and held it cuz his other businesses are going through a transition." **Net: deposit secured, full balance pending — technically an open/half-close.** Carson's honest framing: *"even if you call this call a no-close, I'm sure you learned a thing or two about sometimes how some calls go. This is just how it is."*

**Why this call is the most instructive in the batch:**
- It shows a **genuinely under-qualified prospect** (can't afford it, in financial "code red") and how a skilled closer keeps the deal alive WITHOUT discounting — by isolating the objection, reframing money, and restructuring payment terms.
- It demonstrates that **dropping price up front + speaking the niche's lingo** does most of the heavy lifting.
- It's brutally honest that even a great call can end as a **deposit-only soft close**, not a clean win.

## 6) QUALIFICATION & RED FLAGS

### Who is a GOOD client (Carson's filters)
- **High transaction value / high margin** so the ROI math works: "stem cell clinics, which is very high-end medical treatment… I charge a lot because I know I can make them 50, 75, 100 grand back." On the live call: ED treatment = $5,300, $3,500 profit each.
- **Already has money & an existing painful problem** (the two non-negotiables): "you need to come down to solving a specific painful problem that businesses have, and they already have money."
- The **3 universal problems** every local business has (his qualifying lens): wants more **leads**, more **booked appointments**, better **closing** ("a lot of business owners actually suck at sales").
- **Niche you have a personal background/relationship with** — driven home by the live call: "pick the niche that you already have the personal relationship with or the background with… you can just speak their lingo." The reason the clinic owner warmed up: "No one else is talking to me like this."
- **Red ocean > blue ocean for beginners:** start in a *proven/"saturated"* niche (real estate, roofing, HVAC) — "saturated just means it's proven… lower risk." Blue ocean (carpet cleaners, dog breeders, fencing) = higher risk/reward, less proof.

### RED FLAGS / who he walks from (or de-prioritizes)
- **Can't actually afford it** is the live-call red flag — note he *didn't* hard-walk, he financed; but Carson's voiceover frames it as a weaker deal: "he just does not have the credit history, the money in the bank… due to all these other random scenarios going on." The deposit-only outcome is the cost of working a marginal-fit prospect.
- **Businesses where you can't drive trackable revenue** = structurally bad clients. His entire anti-thesis: web design, n8n automations, "you're saving them time but you're not making them money… that means they're not dependent on you. You're just a nice-to-have."
- **Selling cheap services to broke buyers** ("have fun selling 100 clients at $100 doing all that fulfillment for not much money") — a red flag about the *model*, which informs who he targets.
- **Self-disqualifying low-volume outreachers:** on the VA point — if *you* can't book "2, 3 appointments minimum a week," you're not ready to scale/delegate.

## 7) CLOSING TACTICS, URGENCY, RISK-REVERSAL & GUARANTEES

### Closing tactics observed
- **Price-first disarm** (see §5.5) — removes the biggest mental objection before it can fester.
- **Isolation close:** "If money were out of the equation, would you do this?" — separates *can't-afford* from *don't-want*, so you know exactly what you're solving.
- **Trial-close to lock the goal:** "With those small changes, do you think you could reach that easily?" → gets a yes on the outcome before talking commitment.
- **Commitment transfer:** "I can help with the investment, but I can't help with the commitment. That has to come from you." — puts the decision back on the prospect without pressure.
- **Payment restructuring instead of discounting:** break $15k → $1,300/mo financed; never lower the headline value.
- **Same-day onboarding to kill cold feet:** "we've closed someone in the morning, we onboard them with our client success manager the exact same night… because we want to close the gap between when they're second-guessing the decision." (`9TRPg0Rj1i8`)

### Urgency
- **Quantified cost-of-inaction**, not fake scarcity: showing the clinic was burning money every week it waited (overhead > revenue) made delay the expensive option.
- **"You didn't get into this as a hobby"** — reframes hesitation as betraying their own goal: "you didn't come here to not make money, you came here to make money, right?"

### Risk-reversal / guarantees
- **For beginners (outreach):** explicit **pay-per-result / free-trial** risk reversal — "we will get you new jobs on a pay-per-job basis. You only pay me when we close a job… it's very hard for people to say no because it sounds like there's no risk." Plus "$300 for 3 weeks" trials.
- **On the premium call:** the risk-reversal is **shared-skin framing + the keep-forever AI asset**, NOT a money-back guarantee: "We've all got skin in this game. My success is your success long term," and the AI caller "is something you keep beyond even working with us."
- **The deposit mechanic** is risk-reversal *for the client's* business (kills no-shows) — used as a selling point, and Carson takes a deposit himself to secure the deal.
- **Notable:** no explicit performance/money-back guarantee is offered on the premium call. The reassurance is competence + ROI math + the retained asset.

## 8) Quick-Reference Cheat Sheet

- **Acquisition priority:** Paid ads (8–10 calls/day) > Outreach (beginner/free) > Organic content.
- **Outreach golden rule:** pre-warm 3–5 posts over 3–5 days, then a 2–3 sentence convo-opener — "open a conversation, don't pitch-slap." 50–100 DMs/day, follow up 3–4× over ~7 days.
- **Pitch:** 3 pillars on paper — (1) Lead gen via ads, (2) Appointment setting via fast setters + AI caller + **deposits**, (3) Closing/script. "Business owners don't care how you built the AI — they care about leads, appointments, revenue they can track."
- **Pricing:** $10–25k one-time / 90 days, PIF or financed, + client-funded ad spend; upsell retainers later. Drop the price EARLY. Anchor on 3–5× ROI + the keep-forever AI caller.
- **Objections:** isolate ("if money were out of the equation…"), reframe money as a *symptom* not a problem, transfer commitment to the buyer, restructure payments instead of discounting.
- **The $15k difficult call:** prospect couldn't afford it → didn't discount → isolated + reframed + financed to $1,300/mo → **deposit secured, balance pending**. Honest that some great calls still end as soft closes.
- **Qualify for:** high-margin niche + existing money + painful trackable problem + a niche whose lingo you can speak. Walk from: no-trackable-revenue services, broke buyers, cheap-service models.
