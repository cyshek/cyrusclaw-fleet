## His method, told straight (the respectful version)

**What he trades.** Intraday US day-trader. Primary instrument is **SPY, via 0DTE options** (puts and calls), in large size (~900 contracts on the day he narrates). Also Tesla, Amazon, ES futures. Notably he has *stopped scanning* — over the last few years he trades a **fixed, familiar basket** so he can build deep context on a few names rather than chase pre-market movers. Execution charts are 5m/15m, but the actual decision is made on the **order book** (DOM, Level 2, time & sales, footprint, Delta).

**His theory of edge.** The market is an auction. Price doesn't have "more buyers than sellers" — every trade has both. What matters is *who is aggressive* (market orders, lifting the offer / hitting the bid) vs. *who is passive* (resting limit orders). Supply/demand zones and key levels tell you **where** to pay attention; they are **not** a signal by themselves. He is explicit and repeated about this: he *rejects* mechanical pattern trading ("it hit this level twice so it goes down" is, in his words, wrong). The trade is taken only when the tape confirms who is winning the auction at that level.

**Concrete order-flow tells he uses:**
- **Delta** (net aggressive buy vs sell). Strongly negative Delta = aggressive selling.
- **Effort vs. reward / absorption:** aggressive selling *into* a demand zone but price *holds* = a passive buyer is absorbing the supply → reversal up (and the mirror image for tops). His iPhone analogy: if you keep cutting the price and someone keeps buying it all up, a big passive buyer is there.
- **Unfinished auctions** (a zero on one side of the footprint at a high/low) → price tends to return to finish them, and *then* may reverse. He's careful: this only applies at the day's highs/lows, not mid-range.
- **Exhaustion** (a big move where Delta stops expanding even as price ticks up) → momentum is gone.
- **Volume confirmation:** rising price on rising volume = real interest; rising price on flat/falling volume = no interest / possible trap.

He is emphatic this is **"an art… the intuitive side of reading the tape… it takes very long and requires screen time."**

**Named setups (he treats these as *contexts*, not triggers):** Morning Top Reversal, Opening Range Drive, Second Day Play, Continuation Sell-off, Midday Reversal. Each is a *situation* that "ignites interest"; he then waits for order-flow confirmation before acting. He also bakes in a **time-of-day** view (first ~30 min sets institutional tone; ~10:00 ET data/sentiment shift is a window he avoids).

**Risk & money management (his most concrete, rule-like material).**
- Stop goes where the **thesis is invalidated** (price proves you wrong) — never an arbitrary dollar figure.
- **1–2% risk per trade** while you're still finding profitability; keep risk *constant* until you've been consistently profitable for ~a year.
- **Position size = dollar-risk ÷ distance-to-stop** (e.g., $10 risk, $1 stop → 10 shares).
- Only take trades with **≥ 2R** potential. The setup "ignites" interest; the favorable R is what "attracts" the trade.
- His win-rate math: at **2R you're profitable at 50%** win rate; even **~40%** works with a good R. "You don't need to be right all the time."
- **Working capital ≠ risk:** capital deployed is not the loss; the stop is.
- Diagnostic: realized **R worse than −1R consistently → you're not honoring your stops** (an execution failure, not a strategy failure).
- **Never up-size right after a big win or a big loss** (ego/revenge control). Scale up only with sustained profitability *and* a high-quality setup.
- Hard daily discipline: after **2 losers don't up-size; after 3, step away.**
- **Journal every trade by R, not P&L.** A +$20K trade that risked $20K (1R) is a *bad* trade.

**Psychology & process (which he says is the decisive half).**
- A **20–30 min pre-market game plan every day** (key levels, prior-day action, economic/Fed/earnings events, written IF/THEN hypotheses) — plus a personal rule that he is *not allowed to trade* if he isn't prepared at the desk 30 min early.
- **Tag every trade to a named playbook setup; log "missed trades"** for replay/learning (this is literally what TradeZella does).
- **Recap every trade:** what was done well / wrong, sold too early?, what was missed.
- Measure **progress by process metrics** (win-rate, R, loss-cutting, plan accuracy), *not* monthly P&L — especially early.
- Ignore social-media trader-flexing (manufactures FOMO → bad trades); pick **one** style/mentor and stop strategy-hopping.
- **Meditation + journaling** cited as the two biggest turning points in his career.
- He ranks **intuition** as a key component — explicitly pattern-matching built from ~10 years of screen time ("I've seen this exact thing before"). He frames mastery as a multi-year marathon.

The throughline, in his own losing-trade post-mortems: **the setup was usually "right"; the money was lost to fear, late entry, tilt, revenge, or not honoring the stop.** By his own account the alpha lives in *execution and psychology*, not in the signal.

## The honest split

### ✅ MECHANIZABLE / TESTABLE on our harness (OHLCV bars)

These are real and worth stating plainly — but note they are overwhelmingly **risk/money-management and process**, plus a few weak signal *proxies*. None of them is his actual entry edge.

1. **Expectancy / win-rate math (2R ⇒ profitable at 50%).** Pure arithmetic; trivially confirmable. Useful as a sizing/expectancy sanity-check module, not as alpha.
2. **Fixed-fractional sizing (1–2% risk; size = risk ÷ stop-distance) vs. ego/streak sizing.** *This is the single most bench-able thing he teaches.* Backtest identical entries under flat-1%-risk vs. ramp-after-wins / shrink-after-losses; compare terminal equity, max drawdown, risk-of-ruin. A genuine, testable money-management hypothesis.
3. **"Never up-size after a big win/loss" beats streak-reactive sizing.** Same machinery as #2 — simulate sizing schemes over a return series, measure drawdown/ruin.
4. **Volume-confirmation filter** (require volume expansion for breakout entries). Testable on bar data *without* footprint: compare volume-confirmed vs. unconfirmed breakouts.
5. **Failed-breakout / "effort with no reward" reversal — as a PROXY.** On OHLCV: breach of a key level that closes back inside within N bars on elevated volume → fade. Testable, but it *loses his actual signal* (Delta/absorption), so expect a much weaker, noisier edge than his discretionary version.
6. **Opening-Range Breakout** (first 15/30 min). Classic, fully testable — but naive ORB's published edge is thin and regime-dependent.
7. **Time-of-day session filter** (e.g., avoid ~09:55–10:15 ET). Testable as a filter on a momentum strategy.
8. **Stop-at-structure + 2R-target exit overlay.** Testable as an exit/management module on top of any entry signal.

### ❌ IRREDUCIBLY DISCRETIONARY (cannot mechanize on our data, and shouldn't pretend to)

1. **The actual entry trigger is order-flow reading** — aggressive vs. passive participants at zones via **DOM / Level 2 / time & sales / footprint / Delta / absorption / exhaustion / unfinished auctions.** This is microstructure data we don't ingest, *and* he interprets it contextually with no fixed threshold. He explicitly rejects mechanical rules.
2. **Context synthesis.** He insists no single candle/footprint/level means anything without the session story. The edge is the *synthesis*, not any one input.
3. **Intuition.** He ranks it as a key component — non-mechanical pattern recognition from ~10 years of screen time.
4. **Discretionary scaling.** Size by conviction/regime ("harder on high-quality setups") — judgment, not a formula.
5. **The "sit on hands" filter.** A large part of his edge is *not trading* when he can't read the tape or isn't prepared. There is no signal to backtest in a discretionary no-trade decision.
6. **Execution under live P&L pressure.** His own data says losing trades had the right setup but failed on execution/psychology. A backtest definitionally cannot model fear, tilt, or stop-honoring.

## Verdict (blunt, for Cyrus)

**Umar Ashraf is the real deal as a discretionary trader, and his edge is genuinely discretionary — it does not reduce to a mechanical signal on the data our bench uses.** His decision-making lives in live order-flow/tape reading + context + intuition, and his *durable* advantage is execution discipline and risk control under pressure. He says this himself, repeatedly and credibly: the setup ignites interest, the tape makes the trade, and most losses are execution failures, not signal failures.

What that means for the bench:

- **There is no "Umar Ashraf strategy" we can faithfully backtest.** Any "failed-breakout-on-volume + opening-range + time-of-day-filter" reconstruction would be a *pale OHLCV proxy* of his real, footprint-driven edge — and would almost certainly underperform him, because we'd be stripping out the exact information (Delta/absorption/DOM) he uses to decide. Presenting such a proxy as "his method, tested" would be intellectually dishonest.
- **The part of him that IS mechanizable is his risk & money-management framework** — fixed-fractional sizing, ≥2R selection, anti-ego streak-sizing, expectancy math, structural stops + R-based exits, journaling by R. That's bench-able and, frankly, *valuable*: it's an overlay that could improve *any* strategy we run, and it's the thing he'd tell you matters most anyway. It is a money-management contribution, not an alpha signal.
- **Bottom line:** if the question is "does Umar give us a testable alpha signal to add to the tournament?" — **no, honestly.** If the question is "is there anything here worth taking?" — **yes: his risk/sizing/process discipline,** which we could encode as a portfolio-level money-management module rather than as a strategy. His real lesson for a quant bench is almost an *anti-lesson*: he's living proof that a large, durable retail edge can be **discretionary execution**, and that the highest-leverage code we could write isn't a new entry signal but better **risk management and sizing** around the signals we already have.

This is a respectful "his edge is real but mostly not mechanizable" finding — exactly the kind of honest answer the task asked for, and a clean input to the wind-down decision.
